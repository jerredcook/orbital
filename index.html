ORBITAL

Everything this map can do. It's really two worlds in one — a live satellite tracker around Earth, and the whole solar system to fly through. Switch any time with ☉ System in the top bar, and Esc always steps back a level.

🛰 The live satellite tracker

The opening view is every active satellite in the public catalog plus the three biggest debris clouds — about 18,000 objects — each flown live from real tracking data, moving in real time.

  • Tap any dot to lock on and follow it. The panel shows its name, catalog number, altitude, speed and orbit; the big, well-known craft (ISS, Hubble, Tiangong…) load a real 3D model as you zoom in.
  • Colour is the orbit type — low-Earth, medium, geostationary, highly-elliptical, debris. The legend (top-left — behind ☰ on a phone) explains it and lets you switch types off to declutter.
  • Search up top by name or catalog number to jump straight to a craft.

📋 Filters, conjunctions & screening

The side panel — tap ☰ on a phone — is the control desk.

  • Conjunctions: every pair of objects passing within 5, 10 or 25 km of each other, found live. Click a row to fly to the close approach.
  • Screen one satellite: select it, then “Screen close approaches” to list everything that comes within 25 km of it over the next day.

📍 What flies over your house

  • Drop a spot on Earth (or use your location) and Orbital works out which satellites pass over it, and exactly when.
  • Naked-eye passes: every pass is checked for real visibility — the craft sunlit overhead while you're down in darkness. Passes you could actually see are marked 👁, there's a "naked-eye passes only" filter, and for the bright ones (the ISS, Tiangong) Orbital counts down to the next sighting.
  • The sky chart: a naked-eye map of what's crossing your sky right now, horizon to overhead.
  • Pass alerts count down to the next good viewing opportunity.

☉ Fly the solar system

Hit ☉ System to leave Earth for a Sun's-eye view: all eight planets on their real orbits, the five dwarf planets — Ceres in the asteroid belt, and Pluto, Haumea, Makemake and Eris out beyond Neptune — famous comets on their dramatic paths, the three known interstellar visitors on their unbound trajectories, the asteroid belt, Jupiter's two Trojan clouds, the Hilda triangle, the major asteroid families, and an accurate star sky behind it all. Tap anything — planet, dwarf planet, comet, interstellar object, moon or spacecraft — to fly over and orbit it, right down to just above its surface.

  • The positions are real — planets, dwarf planets, comets, interstellar visitors, moons and probes are all placed from real JPL ephemerides, not decoration.
  • The moons keep their true configuration. Watch the Galilean moons dance around Jupiter, or Triton circle Neptune backwards. Each probe flies its real orbit's tilt and character too — Juno's dramatic polar ellipse, MESSENGER's stretched loop at Mercury (shapes gently eased so they stay in frame).
  • The dwarf planets and comets ride their real paths — Pluto crossing Neptune's orbit, Eris out past 90 AU on a 44°-inclined sweep, Halley's retrograde 76-year loop, Hale-Bopp's near-vertical dive.
  • Pluto brings its whole family. It wears its real New Horizons map — fly close and you'll see the heart — and all five moons: Charon, half Pluto's size on a steeply-tipped 6.4-day orbit (tap it for the story of the great binary), plus the tiny chaotic tumblers Styx, Nix, Kerberos and Hydra. Pluto and Ceres can even be entered as full navigable globes — Ceres streams NASA's real Dawn mosaic down to ~140 m/px.
  • Three visitors from other stars. ʻOumuamua, Borisov and 3I/ATLAS aren't in orbit at all — they fell in from interstellar space, whipped once around the Sun, and are leaving forever on open, unbound paths (violet arcs, not closed loops). Their eccentricities climb from 1.2 to a wild 6.1 — the clearest sign these came from somewhere else.
  • A planet's panel lets you enter it as a full navigable globe, or Show moon orbits to ring it with its satellites' paths.
  • True scale toggle: trade the legible “orrery” spacing for the real distances and sizes — space is mostly emptiness, and this shows how much.
  • The spacecraft timeline scrubs through the decades — watch the orbiters arrive and depart across mission history.

🌑 Land on another world

Enter a planet or the Moon and you drop onto its actual surface globe — real NASA imagery — to navigate just like you do Earth, from orbit down to the ground.

🔭 The showpieces

Some famous craft never appear in the tracker because they don't orbit Earth — they sit at gravitational balance points or sail through deep space. They're all here, findable by search or a tap:

  • At the Lagrange points: the James Webb out at L2 (and WMAP, retired from there to a Sun-circling orbit in 2010); SOHO, DSCOVR and ACE at L1.
  • Out in deep space: Voyager 1 & 2, Pioneer 10, New Horizons and more — billions of km out, far too far to draw to scale, so each is shown in its true direction with its real distance noted on the card.

🔗 Share what you find & get around

  • The link updates as you explore — copy the address to send someone the exact thing you're looking at, whether a satellite, a planet, a moon or a probe. They'll open right to it.
  • Controls: drag to rotate, scroll or pinch to zoom, Esc steps back one level. The ? button (top-right) reopens the welcome any time.

Live orbits: CelesTrak (Dr. T.S. Kelso). A note on the realism: the planets ride JPL's Keplerian elements; the moons and probes carry real osculating elements from JPL Horizons (epoch 2026 — most accurate in the current era), with the major moons landing within about a degree of the truth. Two honest cheats: probe orbits keep their real tilt but have their shape gently eased so they stay in frame, and the system view is lit by a viewing light rather than the real Sun, so day/night lines out there aren't physical. It's a labour of love — a free page for family and friends. Clear skies! 🌌
